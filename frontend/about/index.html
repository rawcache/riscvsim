Built for engineers who want to understand the machine.

Computer engineering principles are some of the most beautiful ideas in all of technology. StudyRISC-V exists to make them accessible.

Why this exists

The tools weren't there.

If you want to learn Python, you have Jupyter notebooks, hundreds of interactive tutorials, and tools that meet you where you are. If you want to learn RISC-V assembly, the foundation of modern processor design, you get a dense textbook and a simulator that looks like it was built in 2003.

That gap bothered me. The concepts aren't hard. The execution of a single addi instruction, the way a function call builds a stack frame, the elegance of a load-store architecture, these ideas are genuinely beautiful once you see them clearly. The tools just weren't showing them clearly.

I built StudyRISC-V because I was frustrated taking ECE 2035 at Georgia Tech and watching students struggle not with the concepts, but with the tooling. Venus works. RARS works. But neither of them makes you feel like you understand what the CPU is actually doing.

Who built this

Satchit Seth

Computer Engineering · Georgia Tech

I'm a Computer Engineering student at Georgia Tech and a former Senior Product Manager with 12 years of experience building AI/ML-driven products. I built StudyRISC-V to bridge the gap between the hardware foundations that engineers rarely get to see and the software-first world most of us live in.

My background is in product and systems. I've worked on lead scoring, fraud detection, and conversational AI. But the more time I spend in CS and CE coursework, the more convinced I am that understanding the machine, the actual electrons-and-gates level, makes you a fundamentally better engineer at every level above it.

StudyRISC-V is my attempt to make that understanding more accessible. It's free for Georgia Tech students and open to everyone.

The surge of AI makes hardware knowledge more valuable, not less.

Every large language model runs on silicon. Every inference call touches memory hierarchies, cache lines, and instruction pipelines. The engineers who understand those layers will build better systems, debug harder problems, and see around corners that pure software engineers will miss.

Computer science education has drifted toward abstraction. StudyRISC-V is a small correction in the other direction.
